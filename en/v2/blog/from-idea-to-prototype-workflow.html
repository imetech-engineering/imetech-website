From idea to prototype: my workflow

A detailed look at how I approach projects, from initial concept to working prototype.

December 13, 2025 Ivo Mengerink 9 min read

Every engineering project starts the same way: with an idea. But turning that idea into a working prototype requires structure, experience, and the right workflow. In this article, I share my approach to product development.

The first 24 hours: from idea to action

When a new project lands on my desk, the first thing I do is resist the urge to start building immediately. Instead, I spend the first day understanding the real problem. What is the client actually trying to achieve? What constraints exist? What has been tried before?

This discovery phase often reveals that the initial brief only tells part of the story. A 30-minute call can save weeks of rework later.

Week 1: The reality check

Once the problem is clear, I assess technical feasibility. Can this be done with off-the-shelf components, or do we need custom electronics? Is the mechanical design straightforward, or are there tight tolerances? What software platform makes sense?

I create a preliminary project plan with clear milestones, estimated costs, and a realistic timeline. No surprises.

Week 2-4: The first prototype

This is where the real engineering begins. I work iteratively: design a subsystem, build it, test it, improve it. For electronics projects, this means schematic capture, PCB layout, ordering prototypes, and firmware development — often in parallel.

The key principle: fail fast, learn faster. A quick proof-of-concept that validates the core functionality is worth more than a perfect design that takes months.

My toolkit

Over the years, I've settled on tools that maximize productivity:

  • SolidWorks for mechanical design (CSWP certified)
  • Altium / KiCad for PCB layout
  • C/C++ and Python for firmware and software
  • Git for version control on every project
  • 3D printing for rapid mechanical prototyping

When is a prototype ready?

A prototype is ready when it reliably demonstrates the core functionality and provides enough confidence to move to the next phase — whether that's further development, testing with users, or preparing for production.

It doesn't need to be perfect. It needs to answer the right questions.

Conclusion

Product development is not a linear process. It's iterative, sometimes messy, but always rewarding. The key is to maintain a clear structure while staying flexible enough to adapt when reality doesn't match the plan.
